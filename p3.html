How to Sow Pigeon Peas

Optional: Soak pea seeds overnight or scratch lightly with sandpaper.

Ideally, sow seeds in late Spring for an October-November harvest although they may be sowed pretty much anytime.

Sow dried pigeon pea seeds directly into the soil outdoors about 3/4-1 inch deep and approximately 3-4 feet between rows and 2 feet (at least) between seeds.

Plants that are closer together will not grow as large as those with more space, so if smaller row plants are desired, sow closer together. Requires about 140 days from seeding to harvest.

How to grow pigeon peas

Water seeds lightly until sprout appears. Do not overwater.
Continue to water lightly every other day until plant is approximately 12 inches tall.
Water every few days after this as they are very drought-tolerant.

Ideas to share pigeon peas

Pigeon peas are so easy to grow that they can make excellent small potted gifts. They prefer to be transplanted when young since their roots grow deep and may be harmed during transplant, but regardless, be sure a take care with longer roots when transplanting. They also grow excellent from direct-sown seeds in the soil. As a prolific producer, sharing seeds for planting with friends, neighbors and community members is encouraged! They also will propagate from dropped peas, so don’t be surprised if baby “volunteer” trees begin growing. These can be easily mowed over or chopped with a machete and will nourish the soil. The main pigeon pea stem can also be used for fire-starting, able to be shared with neighbors who may have chimineas or firebowls and desire smaller first-starting sticks.

When to harvest pigeon peas

There are options to either harvest and shell the peas fresh or dried. Here are the two methods: For fresh pigeon peas, harvest when pods are bright green and feel full and filled out. They should be firm to the touch. Removing the peas from the pod by hand. Store unused peas in a freezer bag in the freezer for safe storage. For dried pigeon peas, which are much easier to release from the pod, allow the peas to dry in the pod, on or off the bush. When they have turned brown and are dried out, use a plastic bag or a screen to rough the pods against each other, allowing the peas to easily fall from their pods. Working the bag back and forth between your hands should separate pods from seeds easily. Toss the pods back into the garden to enrich the soil and store the loose dried pigeon peas in a jar. They may now be used as any other dried bean or pea.

Health and Soil Benefits

Pigeon peas are known as “nitrogen fixers,” which means theyleave behind nitrogen into the soil in a form useful for plant life and microbes in the soil. For this reason, they make an excellent “green manure,” which is one of the easiest ways to enrich poor soil. Merely cut branches, leaves, or the entire plant and drop around the garden, under trees, etc. As it decomposes, it nourishes the soil. This method is called “chop and dropping,” and is our preferred method of dealing with “weeds,” and other unneeded plants in the garden. Some may grow pigeon peas for their nitrogen fixing capabilities alone!

Pigeon Pea Meal Planning

Pigeon peas have a firm texture and are not apt to become mushy unless cooked deliberately to that point. Cook as you might any other bean, in a pressure cooker, crockpot, or stove top. They are delicious simple with rice for a common dish of “Pigeon Peas and Rice,” or may be cooked, rinsed and used in pasta salads, turned into taco or burrito filling. Other uses for pigeon peas are to grind the dried peas into flour using a pulsing blender or coffee grinder. The resulting corn-meal like textured flour may be used as a “breading” on fried foods like dill pickles or chicken, or even used 1/2, 1/2 with all-purpose flour for baked items like brownies. (We’ve actually made brownies with 100 percent pigeon pea flour and found them delicious!)
